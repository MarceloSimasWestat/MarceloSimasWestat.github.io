Who are the English Learners?

In 2014-15, there were more than 4.8 million ELs across the country. The racial/ethnic composition of ELs differs quite significantly from the overall student population, with Hispanics/Latinos the majority among ELs while White students are the majority across all students.

Overall, the vast majority of ELs are Hispanic/Latino. More than 75 percent of ELs in 2014-15 were Hispanic/Latino, but made up just 25 percent of all students. Asians also comprise larger percentages of ELs than of all students; about 5 percent of all students are Asian, but Asians account for 11 percent of ELs. White students make up the third largest share of ELs at 6 percent.

A comparable percentage of EL and all students have disabilities: 14 percent of ELs are students with disabilities, while 13 percent of all students are students with disabilities.

Where are English Learners?

The following analyses group schools and districts into four categories based on the percentage of students in the LEA or school that are ELs: High (20 percent or more are ELs), medium (5 percent to 20 percent), low (at least one EL student, but fewer than 5 percent), and no ELs. The analyses reveal that, although there are few districts and schools with medium or high percentages of ELs, these districts and schools enroll the majority of ELs. In other words, ELs are concentrated in a relatively small number of districts and schools.

Seven percent of districts were categorized as having high percentages of ELs. These school districts enroll 46 percent of the ELs. Eighteen percent of districts were in the medium category, and these districts also enrolled 45 percent of the ELs. Together, this 25 percent of districts enrolled 91 percent of the ELs in 2014-15, indicating that the majority of ELs were concentrated in a relatively small number of districts.

Similar to the district level, ELs were concentrated in a relatively small number of schools. Just 15 percent of schools had 20 percent or more students who were ELs, but these schools enrolled 65 percent of the ELs in 2014-15. Together with schools with 5 to 20 percent of students who were ELs, 92 percent of ELs were enrolled in 38 percent of schools. In contrast, more than half - 57 percent - of the total number of students enrolled in 2014-15 were enrolled in schools with less than five percent of students identified as ELs.

Languages Spoken by English Learners

The English Learner population in the United States is extremely diverse. ELs speak a wide variety of languages at home, including Spanish, Arabic, Chinese, Vietnamese, and Somali. The most-commonly spoken language varies across the states, reflecting the differences in populations of ELs in each state.

Consistent with the overall percentage of ELs who speak Spanish as their primary language at home, Spanish is the language most commonly spoken by ELs at home in 45 of states and the District of Columbia. In all but three of those states, more than 50 percent of the ELs in the state spoke Spanish at home. Across all 50 states and the District of Columbia, Spanish is one of the top five most commonly spoken languages at home. Alaska (Yupik languages), Hawaii (Iloko), Maine (Somalia), and Vermont (Nepali) are the five states in which Spanish is not the most commonly spoken language by ELs at home.

ENDNOTES

Data Exclusion Rules

Data included in this story may differ slightly from other published reports due to certain data decisions. For the purposes of these analyses, schools with incomplete or inconsistent data values were excluded. Less than 5 percent of schools were excluded for the following reasons:

  1. The school reported incomplete data for enrollment and/or the count of students suspended for at least one race/ethnicity subgroup.
  2. The count of students suspended was larger than the school enrollment for at least one race/ethnicity subgroup. Schools with partial or missing data for English learners were included in all analyses except those that focused on these subgroups.

Preschool Definition

The definition of preschool used in the Civil Rights Data Collection (CRDC) includes preschool programs and services for children ages 3 through 5.

Map Notes

A district might have missing data on the map if:

  1. It did not report suspension data or the data were inconsistent.
  2. It did not match with the NCES district boundary file.
  3. Twenty-five percent of its enrollment was in schools with incomplete or inconsistent data (see Data Exclusion Rules).

District data displayed on the map may not be based on all schools in the district. All districts with data have complete and consistent data for at least 75 percent of their enrollment.

On this map, the label Elementary includes all school districts that are financially responsible for providing education only to children in the elementary grades. Some elementary districts share territory with one or more Secondary districts. The label Independent Elementary refers to those Elementary school districts that are geographically independent of other school districts.

The label Secondary includes all school districts that are financially responsible for providing education only to children in the upper grades. This includes pseudo-secondary school districts created by the Census Bureau.

The label Unified refers to districts that provide education to children of all school ages. In general, where there is a unified school district, no elementary or secondary school district exists.

Other Technical Notes

This report uses three different types of percentages: a suspension rate, an enrollment composition percent, and a suspension composition percent.

  • The suspension rate is the number of students suspended divided by the number of students enrolled. A suspension rate is calculated overall and for each subgroup.
  • The enrollment composition percent is the number of subgroup students enrolled divided by the total number of students enrolled.
  • The suspension composition percent is the number of subgroup students suspended divided by the total number of students suspended.

When calculating the suspension rate for students with disabilities in each racial/ethnic group, suppressed enrollment counts were imputed to zero. Enrollment by race/ethnicity for students with disabilities was suppressed for privacy protection when the count was fewer than three (including zero).
